How much vitamin E should you take a day?

Short answer: Because vitamin E is fat-soluble, the body stores it rather than passing the excess, so taking large amounts isn't automatically better and can be worth caution. The right daily amount is individual, so it's best confirmed with your doctor or pharmacist rather than guessed from a general article.

Vitamin E shows up on a lot of supplement shelves next to omega-3 and vitamin D, and it's easy to assume that if a little is good, a little more must be better. With fat-soluble vitamins, though, that instinct can work against you. Here's a calmer way to think through how much makes sense, without treating any single number as a rule for everyone.

Fat-soluble means it's stored

Unlike water-soluble vitamins that the body tends to pass, vitamin E is fat-soluble and stored in the body. That means very large amounts do not simply wash out, so more is not automatically better and can warrant caution.

It is also absorbed better with a meal that has some fat, so if you take it, pairing it with food is sensible. In practice, that often means people naturally fold it into breakfast or dinner rather than taking it on an empty stomach first thing in the morning — not because one time of day is objectively correct, but because it lines up with a meal that already has some fat in it.

Because the body holds onto fat-soluble nutrients rather than clearing the excess quickly, it also makes sense to think about your total intake across everything you take, not just one bottle in isolation. If you're already taking a multivitamin, a skin-focused supplement, or anything else that lists vitamin E on the label, it's worth glancing at how those add up rather than assuming each product exists in its own silo.

Confirm the amount, then stay steady

Because the right amount depends on your diet and situation, it is a good question for your doctor or pharmacist rather than a number to pick yourself. Many people already get vitamin E from food — nuts, seeds, and certain oils are common sources — which is worth factoring in before adding a supplement on top.

A practical approach some people find reassuring is starting on the lower end of whatever a professional recommends, rather than the highest number they've seen mentioned somewhere, and only adjusting from there after checking in again. Since everyone's diet, health background, and any other medications or supplements differ, what works well for someone else isn't automatically the right fit for you.

Making it part of an everyday routine

One of the more overlooked parts of any supplement plan is simply remembering to take it the same way, day after day. Vitamin E isn't the kind of thing that needs to be taken at an exact minute, but pairing it with something you already do — a meal, brushing your teeth, making coffee — tends to make it stick better than trying to remember it cold.

If you take other supplements or medications too, it can help to space them out mentally, even loosely, so you're not guessing later whether you already took something. A simple checklist or reminder app removes that uncertainty, and over weeks and months that small bit of structure adds up to far fewer missed or doubled-up days than trying to keep it all in your head.

Frequently asked questions

Can you take too much vitamin E?

Because it's fat-soluble and stored by the body, large amounts aren't automatically better and can warrant caution. Confirm the right amount with a professional.

Should I take vitamin E with food?

Yes — it's fat-soluble, so taking it with a meal that has some fat may help absorption.

Does it matter if I also take other supplements with vitamin E in them?

It can — if a multivitamin or other product already includes vitamin E, that adds to your total intake. It's worth mentioning everything you take when confirming the right amount with a doctor or pharmacist.
