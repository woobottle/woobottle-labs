How can you stop fishy burps from fish oil?

Short answer: Fishy burps are a common complaint with fish oil. Many people reduce them by taking omega-3 with a meal, splitting it across the day, or storing the capsules in the fridge or freezer. If it keeps bothering you, a pharmacist can suggest other forms.

The fishy repeat after an omega-3 capsule is one of the most common reasons people quietly stop taking it — not because it isn't useful for them, but because the aftertaste makes it unpleasant to keep going. The good news is that most people who run into this find it eases off once they adjust a few small habits around when and how they take it.

Simple things that often help

Taking fish oil with a meal rather than on an empty stomach is the first thing most people try, since food may reduce the repeat. An empty stomach seems to let the oil sit closer to the top, which is often when the burp shows up soon after. Pairing the capsule with breakfast or dinner, rather than swallowing it first thing before eating anything, is a small change but one many people notice a difference from within a few days.

Splitting a larger serving into smaller ones across the day can also help. Instead of taking the full amount in one go, some people try dividing it into two smaller doses — say, one with lunch and one with dinner — so there is simply less oil moving through at once. It takes a little more thought to remember twice rather than once, but a daily reminder makes that part easy rather than something to keep track of in your head.

Some people keep the capsules cold in the fridge or freezer, which they find cuts down the fishy aftertaste. The idea is that a colder capsule takes longer to start breaking down, so any burp that does happen tends to arrive later, once you're further along in digestion rather than right after swallowing it. It's a low-effort thing to try for a week or two and see if it makes a noticeable difference for you.

What tends to make it worse

A few everyday habits can make fishy burps more noticeable, even if they're not the root cause. Taking the capsule right before lying down or exercising, for instance, can bring on a repeat sooner simply because of body position. Some people also notice it more on days they've taken it on a rushed, mostly empty stomach — a quick coffee instead of an actual meal beforehand. None of this means anything is wrong; it's just useful to notice patterns in your own routine, since what triggers it can vary a fair bit from person to person.

If it still bothers you

Different product forms behave differently, so if burps persist, a pharmacist can point you toward alternatives that may sit better — things like enteric-coated capsules or different formulations are sometimes worth asking about. Building a steady routine matters here more than it might seem. If a dose gets taken at random times — sometimes with food, sometimes without, sometimes forgotten and doubled up later — it becomes much harder to tell what's actually helping and what isn't. Keeping the timing consistent for a couple of weeks gives you a clearer picture of whether the small changes are working, rather than guessing from one inconsistent day to the next.

There is no need to force something that consistently disagrees with you; it is worth asking for options. A pharmacist or doctor can also help rule out anything else going on, especially if the burps come along with other stomach discomfort that doesn't seem to ease up no matter what you try.

Frequently asked questions

Does taking fish oil with food help with burps?

Many people find taking it with a meal reduces the fishy repeat compared to an empty stomach.

Does freezing fish oil capsules help?

Some people keep capsules cold and find it reduces the aftertaste. Check the label for storage guidance first.

Does splitting the dose into smaller amounts help with burps?

Some people find taking a smaller amount with each meal, instead of one larger dose at once, feels easier on the stomach and reduces the repeat.
