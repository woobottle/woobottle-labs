Can you take probiotics with antibiotics?

Short answer: Many people take probiotics a few hours apart from their antibiotic rather than at the exact same time. Because antibiotics and probiotics interact, spacing them out is a common approach — but your doctor or pharmacist should guide this, especially while you're on a prescribed course.

If you've ever stood at the kitchen counter holding both an antibiotic and a probiotic capsule, wondering whether it's fine to just take them together, you're not alone — it's one of the most common questions people ask their pharmacist mid-course. There isn't one universal rule that fits every antibiotic and every probiotic strain, but there is a habit a lot of people settle into, and it's worth understanding why.

Spacing is the usual approach

Since antibiotics act on bacteria, many people separate their probiotic from their antibiotic by a couple of hours so the two are not taken at the very same moment. This is a widely used habit rather than a strict rule, and the reasoning behind it is fairly intuitive: an antibiotic taken at full strength right alongside a live-culture probiotic may blunt the point of taking the probiotic at all that particular moment. Spacing them out is simply a way of giving each one its own window.

Because you are dealing with a prescribed medicine, the safest move is to follow the specific guidance your doctor or pharmacist gives for your course. Some antibiotics come with their own timing notes — taken with food, on an empty stomach, or at evenly spaced intervals through the day — and a probiotic added on top means you're now juggling three things instead of two. That's exactly the kind of situation where it helps to write your plan down somewhere, even just a simple note on your phone, rather than trying to keep it all in your head after a long day.

It also helps to think about the shape of your day rather than the clock alone. If your antibiotic is a twice-daily dose, morning and evening, a probiotic squeezed in around midday or before bed can create a natural gap without much extra thought. The exact number of hours matters less than simply avoiding the two landing in your system at the very same swallow.

A few people find it easiest to anchor the probiotic to something else they already do at that hour — lunch, a coffee break, brushing teeth at night — so it isn't a floating task competing for attention. Others prefer keeping the bottles physically apart, one by the kitchen and one by the bedside, as a visual cue for which one belongs to which part of the day. Either approach works; the point is finding whatever makes the gap automatic rather than something you have to calculate every single day of the course.

Once your course is finished, some people continue a probiotic for a while — again, your pharmacist can advise what suits you. Starting small if you're new to probiotics

If this is the first time you've taken a probiotic alongside a course of antibiotics, it's common to ease in rather than assume everything will feel exactly as expected from day one. Some people notice their digestion behaves a little differently in the first few days of any new supplement, antibiotics included, and that's part of why pharmacists often suggest starting at the lower end of a dose and paying attention to how you feel before assuming a fixed routine. None of this is a signal to abandon the plan — it's simply a reminder that everyone's experience differs, and a slower, more attentive start rarely hurts.

Keeping a simple log of when you took each dose, and jotting down anything you noticed that day, can make a follow-up conversation with your pharmacist much more useful than trying to recall it from memory a week later. It doesn't need to be detailed — a quick note is usually enough to spot a pattern if one exists.

Frequently asked questions

How many hours apart should they be?

Many people leave a couple of hours between them, but follow the specific advice your pharmacist or doctor gives for your antibiotic.

Should I keep taking probiotics after antibiotics?

Some people do for a period afterward. Ask your pharmacist what makes sense for your situation.

What if I forget and take them at the same time?

Missing the spacing once isn't usually something to worry over, but try to get back to your regular gap for the next dose, and mention it to your pharmacist if you're unsure.
