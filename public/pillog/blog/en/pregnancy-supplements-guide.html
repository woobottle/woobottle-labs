How should you handle supplements during pregnancy?

Short answer: During pregnancy, which supplements to take and how much is something to decide with your doctor or midwife rather than from a general article, since it directly involves you and your baby. Once you've been advised, the everyday part is taking them consistently and with food if suggested.

If you're pregnant and trying to figure out what to take and when, it's normal to feel a little overwhelmed. There's a lot of general advice floating around, and it can be hard to tell what actually applies to your situation. The short version is that this isn't a topic to work out from an article alone — but once your doctor or midwife has given you a plan, there's a lot you can do to make following it easier day to day.

This is a doctor-guided topic

Pregnancy is one of the clearest situations where personalized advice matters. What is appropriate depends on your individual health and stage, so your doctor or midwife should guide the choices rather than a general list. Needs can shift trimester to trimester, and what was fine earlier on isn't automatically fine later, which is part of why this keeps coming back to a conversation with your provider rather than a one-time decision.

General information can help you ask good questions, but it should not replace that professional guidance. It can still be useful to walk into an appointment with a written list of everything you're currently taking, including anything you started before you knew you were pregnant, so nothing gets left out of the conversation by accident.

It's also worth remembering that "natural" or "over the counter" doesn't automatically mean "fine during pregnancy." Some things that were a normal part of your routine before may need to pause, be swapped, or be taken at a different amount for a while. That's not something to guess at — it's exactly the kind of detail your doctor or midwife can settle quickly when you bring it up.

Taking what you're advised, consistently

Some people find that pairing a supplement with an existing habit — like brushing your teeth or eating breakfast — makes it easier to remember than treating it as its own separate task. Others do better with a reminder that lands at a fixed time regardless of what else is happening that morning. There isn't one right way to do this; it's more about noticing what actually works for your days and building around that, rather than forcing a routine that doesn't fit your schedule.

Early pregnancy in particular can bring nausea or changes in appetite that make timing feel unpredictable from one day to the next. If mornings are rough, it may help to ask your doctor whether a different time of day, or taking something with a small amount of food, is reasonable for you. Small adjustments like that are usually easier to sort out once, with guidance, than to keep re-deciding on your own each week.

Keep your doctor or midwife updated as your pregnancy progresses, since advice can change along the way. What's recommended can shift as you move between trimesters or if anything about your health changes, so it helps to treat this as an ongoing conversation rather than a single instruction you follow for nine months without revisiting it.

Making the routine easier to stick with over time

A plan is only as useful as how easy it is to follow on an ordinary, busy day. Pregnancy can already come with enough to keep track of — appointments, symptoms, sleep that doesn't always cooperate — so it helps to keep the supplement part of your routine as simple as possible rather than adding more mental load on top of everything else.

A few small habits tend to help, based on what people commonly report: keeping everything in one visible place instead of spread across different rooms or bags, setting a reminder rather than relying on memory alone, and giving yourself an easy way to glance back and confirm you actually took today's dose instead of trying to recall it later. None of this replaces your doctor's guidance — it's simply about making the guidance easier to follow once it's been given.

If you do miss a dose here and there, try not to let it turn into a reason to give up on the routine altogether. It happens, especially in early pregnancy when everything feels new. The more useful response is usually just to check in with your doctor about what to do next, then get back to your normal schedule rather than dwelling on it.

Frequently asked questions

What supplements should I take during pregnancy?

This is best decided with your doctor or midwife, since it depends on your individual situation and stage.

Can I take my usual supplements while pregnant?

Not all supplements are suitable during pregnancy, so review everything you take with your doctor first.

What should I do if I forget a dose while pregnant?

Missing a dose occasionally happens, especially early on. Rather than guessing what to do, it's best to ask your doctor or midwife how they'd like you to handle it, then get back to your normal routine.
