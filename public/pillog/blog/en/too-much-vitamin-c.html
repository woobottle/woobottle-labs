Can you take too much vitamin C?

Short answer: Vitamin C is water-soluble, so the body tends to pass what it doesn't use, but very large amounts can cause stomach upset or loose stools for some people. Splitting it across the day or taking it with food may feel gentler. For the right amount for you, ask your doctor or pharmacist.

Vitamin C is one of the most popular supplements out there, and it's easy to see why — it's cheap, it's familiar, and it feels like a safe way to give your body a little extra support. But "more is better" isn't really how it works. Here's a calmer way to think about how much you're taking, why your stomach might react, and how a steady routine tends to beat an occasional big dose.

Why more isn't always better

Because vitamin C is water-soluble, the body generally excretes the excess rather than storing it. Still, taking a lot at once can cause digestive discomfort for some people, such as an upset stomach.

Taking it with food, or splitting it into smaller amounts across the day, often feels easier on the stomach.

It's also worth remembering that vitamin C shows up in more places than just a supplement bottle. Citrus fruit, peppers, leafy greens, and fortified drinks all add to the total, and a multivitamin might already include a dose. If you're taking a standalone vitamin C tablet on top of a diet that's already rich in fruit and vegetables, or on top of another supplement that contains it, the amounts can stack up without you really noticing. None of this means you need to track every milligram — it's more a reminder that "how much am I actually taking in a day, from everything combined" is a more useful question than "is this one pill too strong."

What an upset stomach might be telling you

If you've ever taken a supplement on an empty stomach and felt a bit queasy or noticed things move faster than usual afterward, you're not imagining it. For some people, a large single dose of vitamin C — especially without food — can be enough to irritate the stomach lining or draw water into the gut. It's rarely dangerous, but it's uncomfortable, and it can quietly turn someone off from a supplement they otherwise wanted to keep taking.

A few gentle adjustments tend to help: taking it right after a meal instead of before, choosing a lower dose per tablet if that's available, or spacing two smaller doses out during the day instead of taking one large one in the morning. If discomfort continues even after those changes, that's a good moment to check in with a pharmacist rather than just pushing through it or quietly giving up on the supplement altogether.

A steady, sensible routine

Rather than large occasional amounts, a modest daily habit is easier to sustain and to track. Consistency tends to matter more than intensity — taking a smaller amount every day is usually a more realistic long-term habit than taking a large amount only when you remember, then forgetting for a week and trying to "catch up."

How much is right depends on your diet and situation, so let your doctor or pharmacist guide the amount. If you're also taking other supplements or medications, it's worth mentioning vitamin C when you ask, since the full picture of what you take together is what actually matters, not any single pill in isolation.

Building it into your day without overthinking it

For most people, the easiest way to stay consistent isn't a strict schedule down to the minute — it's attaching the habit to something you already do every day. Some people keep their vitamin C next to the coffee maker and take it while breakfast is going. Others prefer pairing it with an evening routine, like right after dinner, when a meal is already in the stomach. Either can work; what matters more is picking one time that repeats naturally rather than trying to remember a new time each day.

It also helps to accept that some days won't go perfectly. If you miss a dose, there's usually no need to double up the next time — just pick the routine back up as normal. A single missed day rarely matters much in the bigger picture, but a habit that feels rigid and unforgiving is more likely to get abandoned altogether after a few slip-ups.

Frequently asked questions

Does extra vitamin C get stored?

Vitamin C is water-soluble, so the body tends to pass what it doesn't use rather than storing large amounts.

Why does vitamin C upset my stomach?

Large amounts at once can cause digestive discomfort for some people. Taking it with food or in smaller portions may help.

Is it better to take vitamin C in the morning or at night?

Either can work. Some people prefer mornings with breakfast so it becomes part of an existing habit, while others find an evening dose easier to remember. What matters most is picking a time you can repeat consistently.
